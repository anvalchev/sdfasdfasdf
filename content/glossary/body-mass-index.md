# Body Mass Index (BMI)

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a measurement used to determine whether an individual is underweight, normal weight, overweight, or obese. It is calculated by dividing a person's weight in kilograms by the square of their height in meters.

BMI is a widely used tool for assessing a person's risk of health problems related to excess body weight. The World Health Organization (WHO) has established the following categories for interpreting BMI:

* Underweight: BMI less than 18.5
* Normal weight: BMI between 18.5 and 24.9
* Overweight: BMI between 25 and 29.9
* Obesity: BMI of 30 or higher

While BMI is a relatively easy and inexpensive way to assess a person's weight status, it does have some limitations. For example, it does not take into account the distribution of body fat or muscle mass. As a result, people with a high muscle mass, such as athletes, may be classified as overweight or obese even though they may not be at an increased risk of health problems.

Additionally, BMI can be less accurate for certain populations, such as older adults or individuals of different ethnicities. For example, older adults may have a higher percentage of body fat even at a lower BMI, while some ethnic groups may have a higher risk of health problems at a lower BMI.

It's important to note that BMI should not be used as the sole indicator of a person's health status.
